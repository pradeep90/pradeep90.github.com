List of Exemplars

Aim

Come up with three exemplars for each concept so that I have concrete cues on which to recall them, instead of some abstract labels I can’t apply.

Why do we need exemplars? So that we can learn to categorize an abstract concept from concrete situations, instead of just working with unproductive, floating labels like “neoliberalism” or “opprobrium”.

Also, list the counter-examples for that category, i.e., exemplars for the alternative values. Like the way “Ubuntu” is an alternative to “Windows” for the variable of closed-source vs open-source operating systems. This way I can categorize every possible value for that variable.

In fact, specify the diagnostic cues for each category. Like for mysterious concepts: the diagnostic cue is whether you have a diagnostic cue (example: elan vital vs not, better performance vs not).

Whenever I need to use or explain a concept, I should refer to its exemplars here. That way I’ll keep this list current and usable. Ditto for when I have a new idea about a concept - I can test it out on these exemplars.

Concepts

Causal models: a = F/m. depression <- rumination about pessimistic explanations vs not. willpower <- energy available in your brain for self-control, your self-control habits.

What are not causal models? Prose explanations are not explicitly causal models. “This could explain the disconnect over cubicles. Maybe the people in charge of facilities, not having any concentration to shatter, have no idea that working in a cubicle feels to a hacker like having one’s brain in a blender.” (PG, Great Hackers) Maybe after reading that, you have a causal model in your head: no concentration to shatter -> no model that [cubicle -> brain in a blender vs not]. And, people in charge of facilities (have no concentration to shatter) -> feel cubicles are good; hackers (have concentration to shatter) -> feel cubicles are bad; hence, a disconnect over cubicles. (Wow, he packed all that in two sentences.)

Similarly, correlations are not causal models. “Obesity is associated with heart disease” is not good enough. You need to build and test the causal model. Similarly for graphs and other representations of correlations.

Value of information: The daily news has pretty much no expected value of information for you regarding what you will eat tonight - it can’t change your meal choice either way (unless it says that your favourite noodles contain toxic chemicals). Call and ask if the gym is open - high expected value of information - if it’s open, then I will go; if not, I won’t bother walking all the way there. With probabilities: let’s say I estimate the probability of the gym being open to be 90%. So, 90% of the time, I expect the person to say that the gym is open. In that case, I would walk to the gym as planned. There would be no change in my plan. But, if he said that the gym is closed, then I won’t waste my time walking all the way and back. I’d stay at home and save the cost of walking. Since I expect this to happen 10% of the time, the expected value of information (I think) is 10% x cost of walking.

I think I can theorize that expected value of perfect information = probability of my original choice being wrong x the value I’d gain by making the right choice instead.

Why? Because when somebody reaffirms your choice, it doesn’t add any value. You would have done it anyway. So, information is only valuable when you think it has a chance of telling you that you made the wrong choice.

Example from How to Measure Anything: Campaign: Success - 80% chance, profit $30 million; Failure - 20% chance, profit -$10 million No Campaign: Success - 100% chance, +$0 million Expected value of perfect information if you decided to do the campaign = 20% chance of it being the wrong choice x $10 million if you didn’t run the campaign = $2 million. Expected value of perfect information if you decided not to do the campaign = 80% chance of it being the wrong choice x $30 million if you ran the campaign = $24 million.

Still not fully clear about expected value of information.

Locality of causality: The error must be close to the point where the program crashed. The mould must be caused by something close to the kitchen stove. The pain in my back must have been caused by something I did recently.

Alternative hypotheses: panic attacks are caused by biochemical problems vs wrong interpretation of bodily symptoms. India will win vs the other team will win (that’s hardly a hypothesis). The part of the rat’s brain they discovered was the pleasure centre vs the desire centre.

Information: 12-ball weighing problem - each message from the weighing machine (left heavier, equal, right-heavier) gives you information. The simple warship game gives you two possible messages: you’ve hit a warship or you’ve not. The news tells you who won the elections.

Entropy and uncertainty: You estimate a certain amount of entropy for each ensemble of messages, like in the ball-weighing game, where you have a maximum of ~1.58 bits of entropy about the set of three messages (when they seem equally likely). In a program with 10 binary variables where you don’t know anything about its current state, you have 2^10 x 1/2^10 x log(2^10) = 10 bits of uncertainty about the current values. In the ball-weighing game, your total uncertainty is log2(24) = ~4.58 bits and you estimate ~1.58 bits of entropy at best from each weighing, so you expect it to take no less than ~2.89 weighings at best. So, you can’t expect to do it in two weighings - an adversary can set up the balls so that your judgment fails.

Categories: Robins and crows seem like prototypical birds; ostriches don’t. But they still seem more bird-y than Abraham Lincoln. In baseball, we have different categories for curve balls and fast pitches. We have movie genres like comedy, action, biopic, and drama.

Empiricism and naive realism: You think there’s no chilli powder left in the cupboard. But you go and check anyway, and what do you find? You think you’ve been using some technique a lot, but then you start actually keeping count… and the results are not pleasing. You fight with somebody else over how likely it is that some politician will get elected, but you don’t realize that probability exists in the mind, not in reality. You think some problem is really hard to solve, but it is just that you think it is complex and hard to solve. It may not actually be so.

Taboo: “boundaries” = property rights = things for which only you can say yes or no, because you’re willing to pay the price to make it happen; things whose value to you exceeds their opportunity cost. “information” = amount by which this message multiplies your confidence in the correct hypothesis. “falsifiability” = being able to recognize this value or its alternatives in the real world, by expressing the abstract concept in terms of concrete sub-concepts (example: “that was a marvellous cover drive” - what isn’t a marvellous cover drive?).

Look only at the differing predictions: Laptop keeps crashing because of overheating due to dust or overheating due to graphics card problem - well, if it crashes even after cleaning the dust, it can’t be because of the dust. Between the spontaneous generation of life theory and life generates life theory: Pasteur prevented living things from coming in contact with the broth and showed that maggots wouldn’t form on its surface. Differing prediction between biochemistry theory of panic attacks and cognitive theory of panic attacks: can you prevent a panic attack even in the presence of sodium lactate?

What would falsify your hypothesis: If you predicted that stocks would go up, then if they go down, your theory is wrong. “The Dark Knight is the best movie ever” - what would falsify this? What if it isn’t at #1 on the IMDB list? What if you’ve watched it fewer times than movies you say you like less, for example, The Terminator? If you say Dhoni is the best finisher in the world right now, but he has a T20 strike rate of only about 120, then is your theory wrong? What would you have predicted? Seriously? You’re not surprised that his strike rate is lower than that of Kohli or just about any other half-decent T20 batsman?

mysterious answers (variables whose values you can’t or don’t test): phlogiston vs not; God is omniscient and benevolent vs not; Wulky Wilkinsen is post-utopian vs not; shows colonial alienation vs not; elan vital vs not; whether something is conscious or not. Examples of non-mysterious things (aka variables): is this the square of a number or not (well, it’s an easily comparable number)? “salacious” vs not; “opprobrium” vs not; how long will a ball take to reach the ground? (The point is not that there’s a formula to get t from h and g. The point is that we can falsify a prediction for t; If you say it takes 5 seconds, but it takes 3 seconds, I can tell that you’re wrong.)

decisions: should I go to bed now or not? what shall I have for dinner - cooked meal, pizza, or chaat? shall I catch this crowded bus or wait for another one? non-decisions: “this is a laptop” is not a decision, it’s just the value of a variable (laptop vs not). “Smoking causes cancer” is not a decision. Also, when standing at the bus stop, “shall I now eat a burger or fly a plane?” is not a decision - those options are not available to you right now. I guess you call it a decision when you consider the choices to be part of a single variable.

variable vs not a variable: Qwerty is not a variable, it’s a category, the value of a variable; smoking causes cancer is not a variable, it’s a branch of a causal model; more food is better than less, all else being equal - is not a variable. iPhone vs Galaxy Note forms a variable (smartphone); straight drive vs leg glance vs … is a variable - which stroke to play; 30 kph or 50 kph is a variable.

value of a variable: keyboard layout - Qwerty vs Dvorak; sabotage vs no sabotage; witch vs not a witch;

decoupling or orthogonality (as opposed to un-orthogonal): The sort function is decoupled from the function calling it, given its type signature and QC property - you can use it without knowing about its other callees. The controls of a helicopter are apparently un-orthogonal: you can’t use one without knowing the state of another; it seems that the different important parts of a helicopter all depend on several controls, so when you change even just one control, all the parts change; God help you if change more than one control at the same time. When you lie to many people who know each other, you can’t tell one person a new lie without knowing the previous lies you’ve told either them or the people they know; however, when you tell the truth, you can just answer their question without thinking about the past. Therefore, the lie you tell depends on not only the truth but also the lies you’ve told and the lies you expect to tell.

fun vs boring (to whom?): Things that are considered fun by the people I know - going out to an “exciting” new restaurant (like a fancy buffet or Arabic restaurant) vs the same neighbourhood restaurant, watching new movies, listening to hot new songs, getting excited about movie trailers, travelling to the beach, mall, or other cool location in a motorbike or car, binge-drinking at home, doing weed, hiking, flying an aeroplane, bungee-jumping or other adventure sports, cooking fancy dishes whose pics you can upload on FB, shopping, and, of course, hanging out with members of the opposite sex.

Things considered boring by the people I know - watching the same movie more than once, especially with friends, doing the same thing two outings in a row, staying at home on weekends, reading books, eating the same meal again, listening to songs or watching movies that are not currently popular, sitting and studying for long periods of time, talking about “serious” matters (like money, studies, philosophy of life - what you should do, what gives you meaning, the futility of consumerism, global warming, sustainability, rationality, important scientific ideas about willpower and positive psychology).

Diagnostic cue: Not doing the same thing again. New movies, restaurants, or songs, not old ones.

It’s like they’re conquering a map of “popular activities” and they want to expand their territory as much as possible. Traversing the same territory (activity) over and over doesn’t help them. Note: Their happiness doesn’t have anything to do with it. I’ve seen people obsess over movie trailers and repeatedly get disappointed after the actual movie (Superman comes to mind). They would do better by watching classic, tried-and-tested movies.

Also, perhaps there’s an opportunity cost to doing things that make you happy. Watching old movies may make you happy, but you miss out on the chance to watch a new one and keep up with all your friends. Maybe it’s all Fear Of Missing Out (FOMO).

Wait. Think in terms of status and tribes. If we accept the hypothesis that humans want to seem high-status within their various sub-tribes, then they would want to display the pertinent signals of high status. Like watching all the latest cool movies, visiting cool locations and uploading pics on FB, typing witty comments on Whatsapp, etc. Maybe it’s all an elaborate status contest. Basically, they will want to mimic high-status individuals and distance themselves from low-status individuals. In particular, the actual benefits of the activity don’t enter the picture at any point. Test this hypothesis.

Question: Isn’t this idiotic? Should you respect this?

decision-oriented thinking: How deep should your understanding of your laptop be? Should you understand the physics on which your silicon processor runs? Why not? Next, how long should you study a viral disease or a car engine? Only as much as you need to make your decisions (about quarantining carriers). You have two information sources - a textbook and a news article; which one should you read if you only have time for one? The one that you expect to change your decision the most.

How to judge the importance of a medium? Look at the decisions it changes. How many decisions have you changed because of a tweet? Depends. If someone tweets about emergency instructions during a flood or about a blood shortage at a hospital, then you might take action. Otherwise, it’s just entertainment most of the time.

Contrast this to the thinking that you have to read a book from cover to cover. Or that you have to collect data meticulously, without spilling a single drop. Or that you have to consider all data to be equal, without privileging any of it over the other, like in a scientific journal, where you’re given a mass of graphs and statistics, with no natural order.

Anytime you invest your time and money in information sources without considering their value, you’re failing to think in terms of decisions.
