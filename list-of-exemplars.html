List of Exemplars

Aim

Come up with three exemplars for each concept so that I have concrete cues on which to recall them, instead of some abstract labels I can’t apply.

Why do we need exemplars? So that we can learn to categorize an abstract concept from concrete situations, instead of just working with unproductive, floating labels like “neoliberalism” or “opprobrium”.

Also, list the counter-examples for that category, i.e., exemplars for the alternative values. Like the way “Ubuntu” is an alternative to “Windows” for the variable of closed-source vs open-source operating systems. This way I can categorize every possible value for that variable.

In fact, specify the diagnostic cues for each category. Like for mysterious concepts: the diagnostic cue is whether you have a diagnostic cue (example: elan vital vs not, better performance vs not).

Whenever I need to use or explain a concept, I should refer to its exemplars here. That way I’ll keep this list current and usable. Ditto for when I have a new idea about a concept - I can test it out on these exemplars.

Concepts

Causal models: a = F/m. depression <- rumination about pessimistic explanations vs not. willpower <- energy available in your brain for self-control, your self-control habits.

What are not causal models? Prose explanations are not explicitly causal models. “This could explain the disconnect over cubicles. Maybe the people in charge of facilities, not having any concentration to shatter, have no idea that working in a cubicle feels to a hacker like having one’s brain in a blender.” (PG, Great Hackers) Maybe after reading that, you have a causal model in your head: no concentration to shatter -> no model that [cubicle -> brain in a blender vs not]. And, people in charge of facilities (have no concentration to shatter) -> feel cubicles are good; hackers (have concentration to shatter) -> feel cubicles are bad; hence, a disconnect over cubicles. (Wow, he packed all that in two sentences.)

Similarly, correlations are not causal models. “Obesity is associated with heart disease” is not good enough. You need to build and test the causal model. Similarly for graphs and other representations of correlations.

Value of information: The daily news has pretty much no expected value of information for you regarding what you will eat tonight - it can’t change your meal choice either way (unless it says that your favourite noodles contain toxic chemicals). Call and ask if the gym is open - high expected value of information - if it’s open, then I will go; if not, I won’t bother walking all the way there. With probabilities: let’s say I estimate the probability of the gym being open to be 90%. So, 90% of the time, I expect the person to say that the gym is open. In that case, I would walk to the gym as planned. There would be no change in my plan. But, if he said that the gym is closed, then I won’t waste my time walking all the way and back. I’d stay at home and save the cost of walking. Since I expect this to happen 10% of the time, the expected value of information (I think) is 10% x cost of walking.

I think I can theorize that expected value of perfect information = probability of my original choice being wrong x the value I’d gain by making the right choice instead.

Why? Because when somebody reaffirms your choice, it doesn’t add any value. You would have done it anyway. So, information is only valuable when you think it has a chance of telling you that you made the wrong choice.

Example from How to Measure Anything: Campaign: Success - 80% chance, profit $30 million; Failure - 20% chance, profit -$10 million No Campaign: Success - 100% chance, +$0 million Expected value of perfect information if you decided to do the campaign = 20% chance of it being the wrong choice x $10 million if you didn’t run the campaign = $2 million. Expected value of perfect information if you decided not to do the campaign = 80% chance of it being the wrong choice x $30 million if you ran the campaign = $24 million.

Still not fully clear about expected value of information.

Information: 12-ball weighing problem - each message from the weighing machine (left heavier, equal, right-heavier) gives you information. The simple warship game gives you two possible messages: you’ve hit a warship or you’ve not. The news tells you who won the elections.

Entropy and uncertainty: You estimate a certain amount of entropy for each ensemble of messages, like in the ball-weighing game, where you have a maximum of ~1.58 bits of entropy about the set of three messages (when they seem equally likely). In a program with 10 binary variables where you don’t know anything about its current state, you have 2^10 x 1/2^10 x log(2^10) = 10 bits of uncertainty about the current values. In the ball-weighing game, your total uncertainty is log2(24) = ~4.58 bits and you estimate ~1.58 bits of entropy at best from each weighing, so you expect it to take no less than ~2.89 weighings at best. So, you can’t expect to do it in two weighings - an adversary can set up the balls so that your judgment fails.

harsh tests (high entropy): Test for the square function: square 2826 == 7986276 - I have no clue whether my function will give that answer, so if it does, that’s super-strong information.

Expected uncertainty reduction: Ball-weighing example - what was your initial uncertainty? 24 possible hypotheses (ball 1 heavier, ball 1 lighter, and so on for 12 balls) all of which seem equally likely to you. So, 24 x 1/24 x log2(1/24) = ~4.59 bits of uncertainty. The first ball-weighing with 4 balls per pan gives you ~1.58 bits of entropy. What is your expected uncertainty after that? Is it ~4.59 - ~1.58 = 3 bits? (Note: log2(24) - log2(3) = exactly 3 bits)

Take the message “left pan lighter than right pan” - you’ve now eliminated the hypotheses where the odd ball was outside the pans, or where a heavier ball was on the left side or a lighter ball on the right side. So, that’s 8 + 4 + 4 = 16 hypotheses eliminated, leaving 8 hypotheses. Your uncertainty now is -log2(1/8) = 3 bits. Similarly, for the message “left pan heavier than right pan”, you eliminate 16 hypotheses and are left with 8 hypotheses for an uncertainty of 3 bits. Now for the message “both pans are equal” - you eliminate the hypotheses that say the odd ball is on one of the pans, leaving the 8 hypotheses about the balls outside, for an uncertainty of 3 bits. Thus, after getting the first message, you expect an uncertainty of 3 bits no matter the message. So, expected uncertainty = 3 x 1/3 x 3 = 3 bits.

Well, in the extreme case, you expect that N messages of entropy U/N will reduce a total of uncertainty of U to zero. So, entropy seems like it is expected uncertainty reduction. You have to consider the different messages and your uncertainty in those possible worlds. My only problem is that I’m not able to derive this mathematically.

decoupling or orthogonality (as opposed to un-orthogonal): The sort function is decoupled from the function calling it, given its type signature and QC property - you can use it without knowing about its other callees. The controls of a helicopter are apparently un-orthogonal: you can’t use one without knowing the state of another; it seems that the different important parts of a helicopter all depend on several controls, so when you change even just one control, all the parts change; God help you if change more than one control at the same time. When you lie to many people who know each other, you can’t tell one person a new lie without knowing the previous lies you’ve told either them or the people they know; however, when you tell the truth, you can just answer their question without thinking about the past. decision-oriented thinking: How deep should your understanding of your laptop be? Should you understand the physics on which your silicon processor runs? Why not? Next, how long should you study a viral disease or a car engine? Only as much as you need to make your decisions (about quarantining carriers). You have two information sources - a textbook and a news article; which one should you read if you only have time for one? The one that you expect to change your decision the most.

How to judge the importance of a medium? Look at the decisions it changes. How many decisions have you changed because of a tweet? Depends. If someone tweets about emergency instructions during a flood or about a blood shortage at a hospital, then you might take action. Otherwise, it’s just entertainment most of the time.

Contrast this to the thinking that you have to read a book from cover to cover. Or that you have to collect data meticulously, without spilling a single drop. Or that you have to consider all data to be equal, without privileging any of it over the other, like in a scientific journal, where you’re given a mass of graphs and statistics, with no natural order.

Anytime you invest your time and money in information sources without considering their value, you’re failing to think in terms of decisions.

So I hope it is self-evident that reframing seemingly-paradoxical probability problems as decision problems generally makes them trivial, or at least agreeably solvable and non-paradoxical. What may be more controversial is that I claim that this is satisfactory not as a circumvention but as a dissolution of the question “What probability should be assigned to x?”, when you have a clear enough idea of why you’re wondering about the “probability.” Can we really taboo concepts like “probability” and “plausibility” and “credence”? I should certainly hope so; judgments of probability had better be about something, and not just rituals of cognition that we use because it seems like we’re supposed to rather than because it wins.

…

But when I try to replace “probability” with what I mean by it, and when I mean it in any normative sense … then I always find myself talking about optimal bets or average experimental outcomes. Can that really be all there is to probability as degree of belief? Can’t we enjoy, for its own sake, the experience of having maximally accurate beliefs given whatever information we already have, even in circumstances where we don’t get to test it any further? Well, yes and no; if your belief is really about anything, then you’ll be able to specify, at the very least, a ridiculous hypothetical experiment that would give you information about how correct you are, or a ridiculous hypothetical bet that would give you an incentive to optimally solve a more well-defined version of the problem. And if you’re working with a problem where it’s at all unclear how to do this, it is probably best to backtrack and ask what problem you’re trying to solve, why you’re asking the question in the first place.

So when in doubt, ask for decisions rather than probabilities. In the end, the point (aside from signaling) of believing things is (1) to allow you to effectively optimize reality for the things you care about, and (2) to allow you to be surprised by some possible experiences and not others so you get feedback on how well you’re doing. If a belief does not do either of those things, I’d hesitate to call it a belief at all; yet that is what the original version of the Sleeping Beauty problem asks you to do.

chunking theory vs schemata: concrete vs abstract information. For example, they differ on whether memory of chess patterns depends on their position on the board - whether a bishop and pawn in the middle is different from a bishop and pawn in the corner (I don’t know chess).

The former alternative would explain expertise as a product of very specific recognizable perceptual chunks and associated productions that evoke from memory information about their significance. The latter hypothesis would explain expertise as based upon general-purpose schemas whose variables can have different values in different situations.

So, chunks are very concrete pieces of information? I kept thinking of chunks as general-purpose programs. Hmmm… If so, this could make it even more important to think only in terms of concrete examples.

But how are we able to apply concepts across domains? Take the art of skimming books. I can do that whether it’s an economics textbook, science journal paper, or Agatha Christie novel. But the specific cues differ from book to book. In fact, the key question is: what cues do we use to chunk? If those cues are rigid and domain-specific, like the board positions in chess, then we won’t be able to use those chunks even if it’s the same pattern of pieces but in another area of the board. However, if those cues are domain-independent, like “read the first line of the paragraph”, then I can use them on any paragraph (while skimming), no matter the content.

All this tells us is that you can’t use a chunk for something it wasn’t designed to do. The main result here was that chess players don’t represent chess patterns independent of location, because location matters to the game. Chess players could have chosen to represent the patterns regardless of location, but they didn’t. The moral is that we don’t have fine enough control over our chunks to make them ignore variables (like location or colour).

Consequentialism is more fundamental than the “property rights” or “personal autonomy” or other such moral heuristics. Yes, you want to have certain near-injunctions like don’t kill or don’t steal. But at the end of the day, they’re still heuristics. Breaking them may be justified in some situations.

The same holds for the heuristic that you shouldn’t “infringe on others’ property” - by forcing your son to follow a career he doesn’t want, using Dark Arts to persuade a customer to buy a product he doesn’t need (probably all modern-day consumer crap), or wasting your friend’s time by not showing up for a planned outing. Ditto for insulting others (and making them feel insecure), breaking your promises, or ignoring what people say in conversation.

Consequentialism overrules the intuitive moral heuristic that these things are wrong. Yes, a world where your friend wasn’t hurt by your insult or the customer didn’t buy the useless product is better than a world where they did, all else being equal. However, all else is not equal. Maybe you’re going to make money from the customer and use it for some worthy cause that outweighs the (value to you of the) damage done to him. (This still seems like a slippery slope to me, but I’m not able to justify it formally).

Here’s a test: can you not conceive of any situation where breaking your promise or using marketing techniques is the right thing to do? Can the resulting situation be better than any alternatives?

Judge the outcomes, not the actions. You did X (smoked cigarettes, lied, cheated, insulted) - is that good or bad? Well, is the world better off than the alternative, in your opinion? Did you get enough utilons from the cigarettes that it was worth the risk of addiction and lung damage? You insulted people to become more popular - is that good or bad? Depends on what happened? Did you use your power to do good things or bad things? Of course, you have to be honest with yourself and admit it when you were just being stupid, but that’s another thing. Morality is not at fault there; you are.
