Scientific Method Test v3.6

Scientific Method v3.6

Here’s my v3.0 algorithm: Collect evidence, come up with small alternative models, and combine those into big models.

Mission: Collect 10 pieces of evidence.

Resource: The Will Power Instinct, by Kelly McGonigal

Chapter Six: What The Hell

Whether you give in to willpower challenges depends on how guilty you feel.

Key variables: how guilty you feel, whether you give in

Evidence

Correlation: Most commonly used strategies to relieve stress are those that activate the brain’s reward system. But, they are also rated as highly ineffective.

Correlation: Only reliable change from eating is an increase in guilt.

Interventions: When smokers imagine a trip to the dentist, they feel heavy cravings for cigarettes. Binge-eaters and public speech -> crave high-fat, sugary food.

Correlation: real-world stress increases risk of relapse among addicts.

Hypothesis from neuroscience: stress shifts the brain into a reward-seeking state.

Stress hormones also increase the excitability of dopamine neurons. Any temptations you run into will be even more tempting.

Correlation: women worried about their finances shop to cope with their anxiety and depression.

Interventions: most effective stress-relievers - exercising, meditating, etc. least effective - shopping, eating, playing video games, etc. Difference - instead of going for the promise of reward, go for things that boost mood-enhancing brain chemicals like serotonin and GABA, as well as feel-good hormone oxytocin. They also shut down the brain’s stress response and induce the relaxation response.

Intervention: being reminded of our mortality makes us susceptible to all sorts of temptations (presumably because we look for hope and security in the things that promise reward and relief).

Question: How is this different from the hypothesis that being reminded of our mortality trigger stress which makes us seek the promise of relief?

Intervention: shoppers asked to think about their own death -> longer shopping lists, more willing to spend on comfort food, and eat more chocolates and cookies.

Correlation: reports of death on the news -> respond more positively to status goods (because they bolster our self-image and make us feel powerful)

Intervention: watch death scene vs nature documentary -> pay 3x as much for things they didn’t want

Intervention: cigarette pack warnings trigger stress and fear -> more smoking

Correlation: Those who drank more than they intended the previous night -> felt worse in the morning. But, the worse the person felt, the more they drank that night and the night after.

Correlation: many dieters felt so bad about any lapse, that they felt as if their whole diet was blown. Then, what the hell, …

Intervention: make dieters think they’ve gained five pounds -> depressed, guilty, disappointed with themselves -> turned to food

Correlation: What the hell effect (failure -> guilt) in smokers, alcoholics, shoppers, child molesters.

Hypothesis: cycle because give in -> guilt -> seek promise of relief (which intensifies promise of reward) -> give in
