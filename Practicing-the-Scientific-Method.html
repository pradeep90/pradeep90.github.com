Science Practice Design v1.0

Let’s go with my idea that science is about getting evidence and making your causal model more and more precise and accurate. Also, assume that we won’t run any experiments right now. We’ll just use evidence given by some resource.

First, collect the evidence and predictions given by some resource. Then, round up their causal models by asking “why” for all the predictions. Finally, eliminate or refine the models so that you get a final hypothesis that predicts all the evidence.

Future practice ideas

Think only in terms of hypotheses. Every single thing in the world is either a hypothesis, experiment, prediction, or evidence. That’s all. Focus on one particular thing at a time - in one pass, look for hypotheses; in the next, look for experiments; and so on.

Taboo the major words.

Come up with sanity checks for your hypotheses, evidence, predictions, etc. Example: is this a hypothesis or just a prediction? In short, look at the hypothesis that says “I have a hypothesis here” and see whether it stands up to your experiments. Also, what would falsify this hypothesis? What does this predict will not happen?

We get in trouble, I think, when we try to create hypotheses without looking at some concrete evidence. Why? Because it’s easy to just add words that seem like they explain everything but really don’t. Remember, your causal model is only as good as the evidence you have. It’s only the evidence that will make your model precise and let you predict the outcome of different states. In any case, if some part of a hypothesis doesn’t deal with any evidence, then it has no business being there. This is test-driven development, in other words. Get the evidence, show that your current model fails to predict it, and then postulate some extra cause. So, the key is in finding experiments where your current model doesn’t predict correctly.

Remember that a causal model is a state-transition function. Focus on the states and how they change into one another. Don’t simply deal with meaningful-sounding words. When you ask “why”, don’t answer with a cause. See the prediction made aka the final state B. Now, posit a previous state A and a causal model that narrowly transforms A into B (and transforms the initial state into A).

Right now, “state-transition function” is just a buzzword. I don’t know what exactly the states are and how you’re supposed to describe the transition. So, I need to look at some concrete causal models and see how they do it. I suspect they will describe the states using certain variables.

Always remember: science is all about getting highly predictive causal models.

Older Ideas

Major attitude: everything has an explanation! Explain literally everything! Or, rather, anticipation-constrain literally everything! Figure out the cause behind everything. And, of course, when somebody claims that X is the cause, don’t accept it without evidence.

Don’t accept anything without evidence Somebody: “X is true” You: “So you say. Show me the evidence.”

A plan is based on a hypothesis (or, at least, on certain predictions) Successful plans are those that are based on correct hypotheses A plan is only as good as the hypothesis it is based on! Wait. Does this mean that a hypothesis drives a plan? Like, you could derive a plan from a hypothesis? And that, given one hypothesis, you can only have one possible (deterministic) plan? This would mean that choosing between plans is basically about choosing between hypotheses. i.e., Decision-making boils down to Truth-finding. But it always has! Remember, your Prediction Score determines everything! Corollary: Don’t ask which plan you should choose (among several), but rather, which hypothesis is true out of several. Ask not which plan would be most successful (out of several plans that achieve the same ends) Instead, ask what are the differing predictions between the hypotheses that those plans represent and see what the reality has been so far. Corollary: Don’t ask about the plan. Ask about the hypothesis it relies on. What are the assumptions it is based on? What does it expect will happen? Corollary: This implies you don’t just talk about a plan. You talk about multiple plans and choose between them. Remember, it’s meaningless to talk about just one hypothesis. Plans are programs. Hypotheses are programs.

Historical Perspective: Curiosity about how things came to be the way they are Why do we have supermarkets instead of ration shops? Why voting / democracy? Why civil service? Why parliament? These aren’t arbitrary facts. They are tightly governed by the true hypothesis about reality. They are superbly entangled. You shouldn’t be accepting it either way without question. “X happened? Oh, ok”. “X didn’t happen? Oh, ok.” – Wrong! Why does USA have Hollywood and why is it so popular around the world? It doesn’t have more people than Europe (I think)

Remember: Going into a new field (like Category Theory) is like going into the magical world. New rules to figure out and stuff. But the most important thing is that you’ve still got the experimental method, and that’s what matters.

Practicing the Scientific Method v1.0

Your mission is to collect ten causal models. Don’t worry about evidence or alternative hypotheses. Just get their fully-specified causal model. See how they describe the state-transition function. Deliver the models in an essay.

If he makes a prediction, ask why. To start with, see the lead of the paragraph and ask why. That’s all. Example: To be fair, the universities have their hand forced here. Cool. Now, why do they have their hand forced here?

In short, you’ll get around one cause from each paragraph. Do that first. Next, see how you can put those causes together into one model.

Release your essay frequently. Build a rough version first, and then iterate.
