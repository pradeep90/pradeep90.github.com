Chunks (Where do we fail most + Faulty Feedback + What do these perverse incentives engender?):

Acquire techniques, learn them, and apply them.

  • Acquire new techniques wantonly and indiscriminately

    We judge a technique by the amount of excitement we feel for it. Old techniques, in comparison, seem boring. Heuristics and Biases.

    So, we acquire useless techniques and we acquire far more than we need.

    Judge all techniques, past and present, by their empirical performance. Sort them and work on those that are actually most promising.

  • Learn the technique

    We need to judge our grasp of a technique by our performance on various empirical tests.

    But instead, we use several misleading smaller tests.

    Basically, “Can I do this?” on misinterpreted actions in a small subset of the actual scenarios.

  • Apply techniques

    Net Benefits Hypothesis and Affective Death Spiral

    Because you don’t test yourself empirically here, you reduce the pressure on the previous stages. You can get away with loose tests there and the cycle continues.

  • Empiricism = Tackling the Technique Trap (in the area of techniques and performance)

    Mystery completely cleared.

  • Richest form of information transfer - Master Coaching

    Person-to-person. You observe some more skilled person perform the technique.

    The coach gives you some simple instructions. You execute them. He corrects you a bit. You do it some more. He corrects you some more. Then, he raises the level of the game and so on.

    Books are good for accumulating information over the ages and broadcasting them. But they probably don’t contain as much information as Master Coaching does.

  • Important Skill: Set up a quick feedback mechanism for any task.

    If Feedback is King, this becomes top priority.

    Practice doing this on different kinds of tasks.

    Writing - how do I get feedback? For what kinds of things do I get feedback?

    Dental health. Fitness (RHR, etc.). Sleep. Wake-up times. Tackling the Technique Trap.

  • On your Fingertip: The technique doesn’t just have to be in your mind, it has to be on your mind.

    It’s not about being able to recall something (what Spaced Repetition tests), you have to be able to remember to use it in the right situations.

    For example, I should remember to use the Technique Trap when reading (and becoming envious) about other people’s achievements or when seeing that other people have read lots of blog posts and books on this subject. If you ask me what the parts of the Technique Trap are, I can recount them pretty well. It’s just that I don’t remember to use it at the right time.

    Ditto for Chunking it Up from “Your Brain at Work”. I don’t remember to use it when I get overwhelmed by some task.

    It must be there when I need it.

    And not just when I consciously need it, like when I’m solving a relevant problem. It must also be available for creative thoughts, when I can combine it with another thought to solve a problem I wasn’t even aware of.
