The Shallows: Thinking in the Age of the Web

Entry question: Has the Web changed our way of thinking?

Summary and Book Review

The (last two ?) chapters are stinkers. He makes a huge stink and cry about “Taylorism” and the principles of Scientific Management and how Google is being “Taylorist” by measuring behaviour at very fine-grained levels and trying to optimize for its goals.

Instead of using labels such as “Taylorist” or “Scientific Management” (which was used in the early twentieth century to optimize factory workers’ productivity), we should do what PG suggested - ask “Is it actually true?”. Or, in this case, is it actually evil?

Apart from that, the book was quite thought-provoking. I found it clearest to imagine the sea change we’ve gone through when he compared the Oral culture to the Written culture and then that culture to today’s Web culture. When you have three different data points like that, it’s easy to hold them against each other and see the pattern.

Neuroplasticity

The brain changes as you experience life.

In particular, the tasks you do over and over get large neural circuits in the brain dedicated to doing them better and faster.

And if you don’t use a particular skill too much, its circuit is gradually taken over by other circuits that you do use.

Also, your brain likes to do things for which it has built up circuits. So, you do that more, and the brain optimizes that circuit more, and a virtuous cycle ensues. That’s how habits are built up. Good or bad - it makes no difference to the brain. Do it a lot and it will get burnt into your brain.

So, when you use a tool a lot - say, like the Emacs editor or your web browser or your smartphone or even pen and paper - some part of your brain comes to represent that tool and it “becomes part of you”. Working with that tool feels like just using any other part of your body. Neuroscientists have found a lot of evidence for this using monkeys, etc. This gives credence to sayings like “this bat has become an extension of my arm”. It really has, at least in your brain.

One consequence of that is that when that tool is taken away, you suddenly feel uncomfortable, almost handicapped. And just like with really handicapped people, if you don’t use that tool (or limb) much, your brain will assume that circuit for some other purpose. So, people who lose their vision will develop a keener sense of hearing and touch. Ditto for those who stop using a particular editor or programming language or thinking aid.

The Medium vs the Message

Carr contends that people argue at the level of the content of the medium. They talk about whether the content was useful or not. What gets missed out is the box the content came in.

I will say more about this in my next essay, which will be on The Medium and the Message and a book that has a lot to say about it. There, the focus will be on value judgments and how the medium shapes those.

Right now, I’ll simply talk about the skills that we develop due to a medium and, more importantly, the skills we let decay due to neglect.

Distractions

With a physical book you have ZERO distractions. Think about that for a minute. There is NOTHING else you can do apart from reading. You can’t check your email or get a notification about new mails. You can’t get random pings or updates from Facebook. You can’t listen to music in a new browser tab or app. You can’t check the scores of the latest match online. All you can do is read. And yet, that is perhaps all we need.
