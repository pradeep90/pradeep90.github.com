Making Predictions from Hypotheses

Entry Question: How do we make predictions from the hypotheses we have?

Belief-levels

We have seen how to come up with hypotheses and compute their posterior probabilities using Bayes Theorem.

Hypotheses make predictions individually. That’s what they are for. “CSK is the best team” makes the prediction that it will reach the IPL finals this year.

However, you don’t just have one hypothesis in your head. You have several. You have the hypothesis that (A) “CSK is the best team”, but also, (B) “CSK is not the best team”.

Say, you updated on all the evidence using Bayes Theorem. You looked at their performance over the last few years and got a lot of strong and weak evidence. Finally, you have 73% probability that “CSK is the best team” and 27% probability that “CSK is not the best team”.

Those are your Belief-Levels for the two hypotheses.

The tournament is about to start. What is your probability estimate that CSK will reach the finals?

  • A predicts that CSK will reach the finals with 99% probability.

    It is the best team after all. Barring unlucky decisions, it should sail through.

  • B predicts that CSK will reach the finals with 60% probability.

    It is not the best team, true, but it is one of the good teams.

So, what is your final probability estimate? Is it 99% (as per A)? Or 60% (as per B)? Or something else?

Weighted mean

Yup. We have to consider the posterior probabilities. The hypotheses with high posterior probabilities have more say in the final prediction than the others.

Final prediction = Sum of individual predictions weighted by the posterior probability.

Here,

Posterior probability of A x Prediction from A + Posterior probability of B x Prediction from B

= 73% x 99% + 27% x 60%

= 88.47%

That is how we make our final predictions. We consider every hypothesis, in proportion to its posterior.

Odds form

It is even simpler if you use the posterior odds instead of the posterior probability. Remember, our version of Bayes Theorem used odds instead of probability to make calculation a piece of cake.

Say, the posterior odds for A:B are (2:5). The individual predictions of (A:B) for reaching the finals are (15:4).

So, final prediction = (2:5) x (15:4) = (2 x 15 : 5 x 4) = (30 : 20) = (3:2)

You don’t have to convert odds into probabilities at all.
