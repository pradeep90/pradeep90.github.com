The Map and the Territory

Entry Question: What makes your Map closely reflect the Territory?

Map vs Territory

Territory = Set of Events

Map = Predictions for those Events

Example: Who’s gonna win the match tomorrow (Event in the Territory)? My guess: India 80%, Australia 20% (Map).

In reality, only one thing is gonna happen in that event (either India wins or Australia wins), just like there’s only thing in each geographical location in the territory (either a mountain or a river or a forest, whatever). However, you don’t know what is there. So, in your predictions (your Map), you guess.

Prediction = Constrained Anticipation

You cannot say Dhoni will hit a six with 90% probability and then say he will hit a four with 90% probability. If you assign 90% probability to hitting a six, you only have 10% probability left for all the other outcomes. Deal with it.

You have 100% of probability. Spread it out among the outcomes. You cannot have more than 100% of probability in your predictions. That’s what it means to have Constrained Anticipation.

What do you anticipate? What do you expect to see? You cannot expect to see a six and, at the same time, expect to see a four. Your expectations are constrained. You cannot expect to see every possible outcome. Expecting to see one outcome reduces your expectations for another. In other words, you cannot have your cake and eat it too.

What this means is, you cannot say “I told you so” no matter what happens.

You have to leave yourself open to surprise. If some low probability outcome happens, you have to suck it up. You cannot say “Oh… I predicted that too”. No, you didn’t.

This is how you can make “predictions” about stuff in the past too. You just say how your anticipation about those events is constrained.

Reflecting the Territory

Incorporating our understanding of Prediction:

Map = Anticipation-Constraints about the Events in the Territory

Note: your Map could be wrong. You could have the wrong anticipations.

Map reflects Territory = High Prediction Score

You have high Predictive Power for the events in the Territory

Your Map contains your final predictions about the events in the Territory You get it by combining the predictions of all your hypotheses, weighted by their posterior probabilities

So, the Complexities of your Hypotheses have already been incorporated into their posterior probabilities. No need to consider it here. If you have a simple hypothesis that predicts stuff correctly and sharply (say, a causal structure), then it will rule. It will end up having a very high posterior probability and thus dominate your final prediction.

What makes a good Map

Yeah, you would want to have high Prediction Score.

But, informally speaking, how would you know if your current Map is a good one or not?

  • Does it actually constrain your anticipations for those events?

    Are you putting more than 100% probability total for all the outcomes? It happens. Turn on the TV and listen to any news channel.

    “This party could win the elections tomorrow. But then, that party could win too. And maybe, some other party could win too. And maybe, some coalition could win.”

    Next day, when the results arrive, no matter which party wins:

    “I told you so! I predicted it! Yay! In your face!”

    Yeah, right.

    How do you know you are not constraining your anticipation? When you are never wrong (in hindsight)!

  • Do you even know what those events are? What the f*ck are you making predictions about?

    Are you covering the whole territory or just leaving vast blank spots in your map?

You’re only allowed to say you truly know a subject - aka have a Map that truly reflects the Territory - when you can make accurate predictions about all events that are part of that subject. You have mastered a subject when you have a high Prediction Score there.

For example, getting the right answer on nearly all the questions on your Math exams. Being able to deal with nearly any situation that comes up in your job.
