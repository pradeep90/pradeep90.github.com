Mental Model of Concepts

Key Question: What are the most valuable and surprising ideas I have?

Overall Aim

I want to practice and research epistemic rationality at speed. Let’s list the most important concepts for each of those aims.

Research

I want to figure out ways to think better and then tailor them for humans.

  • Thinking is information-processing; use entropy to guide your thinking. Remember that conceptspace is superexponential.
  • Humans seem to use radial categories as the basis of their thinking. We can use these categories to form variables in our causal models. Good categories group things that have mutual information and thus reduce the message length needed to infer their common properties. Reducing the number of states, by abstraction, is one way of reducing the entropy. (TODO: stopped at Notes on Words)
  • Think only in terms of hypotheses and evidence (or causal models and information).
  • Exploit locality of causality.
  • Ask for empirical evidence, start from the evidence, and have at least three concrete examples for every concept
  • Write commentaries on important books and essays
  • I want to write out causal models for important fields. I thought that they were too complex for me to do that or even just read existing models. However, now that we know about domain-independent abstractions and simple words, perhaps it can be done.

Practice

I want to install these techniques in my brain. This, of course, involves testing my skills empirically.

Speed

And I want to do all this quickly, for which I need more mental control (via motivation) and efficiency (via cognitive psychology). I also need to take uncomfortable actions, which requires that I have good boundaries and a strong sense of worthiness.

  • It’s not a real idea unless you’ve published an essay about it. So, my aim is to publish an essay about each of the concepts here.
  • Write an essay everyday.
  • Deliver continuously to get high quality and low cycle time in your projects
  • Set boundaries around things that are important to you. Allot them property rights over your time and resources. For example, set deadlines - allot a certain number of days to a project.

Where to go from here

Write essays about the important concepts above. Practice the techniques like hell. Test them empirically (and locally) - seek a lot of information about their worth. Also, implement techniques that boost your control and efficiency. Once you’ve explained your concepts so far, figure out better ones. Finally, decide the relative importance of these aspects and divide your time accordingly.

Take the whole day tomorrow to fill this list of concepts and, after that, get to work.

Short and Sweet

Name your chunks well. I found that having one phrase to capture each of the three conditions for a Causal Bayesian Network helped me remember them effortlessly (“no screw, ideal, and narrow interventions”). I suspect this is a very powerful technique. Exploit it to remember everything important. Also, find out exactly how it works. Do you need to be able to say the chunk out loud within two seconds? How do we design the hierarchy of chunks?
