Scientific Method Test v1.1

Scientific Method v1.1

Let’s continue using my v1.0 algorithm: Look at each paragraph and get the causal link.

This time, pay attention to the direction of causality. Also, say precisely what effect each cause has.

First, think about whether it is correlation or causation. Then, think about whether one of them causes the other, or whether they have some common causal ancestor.

Mission: Use this on one PG essay.

Time: 1 hour

(Note: A -> B stands for “A causes B”)

Raw correlations and causal links

idea -> importance

correlation between best ideas and how bad they seem initially

variations in startups -> how much our expectations about variation are violated

Rather, the correlation is that when variations in success are low or “reasonably high”, are expectations are not violated; when variations are very high, our expectations are violated.

variation in success, our expectations -> whether violation occurs or not. Specifically, if variation is very high, our expectations are violated; else, they’re not.

What is causing our expectations, then? Just human nature? So, will experience not make any difference at all?

Also, why is there vast variation in startup success?

Per batch, there’s a correlation between % of top winners and % of returns.

turn off your intuitions -> succeed in domain; it’s a necessary condition - if you don’t turn off your intuitions, you won’t be successful at all.

Correlation: taking enough risks is hard. I think it is: turn off your intuitions -> take more risks than the average bear.

And that is hard because turning off your intuitions is hard. Why is that, though?

likely to succeed moderately -> fund them

Correlations

how likely they seem to succeed (moderately), funding them; It seems like: how likely -> fund them. But, why does YC feel like funding them when they seem likely to succeed moderately? I think your intuitions play a role too. So: likely to succeed moderately, intuitions -> fund them.

What makes some startups seem likely to succeed moderately? That should also make them actually likely to succeed moderately. seem likely to succeed moderately <- x factor -> be actually more likely to succeed moderately

chances of winning really big, how significant the returns will be; chances of winning moderately, how significant the returns will be. Given that a few startups succeed really big, the % increase in returns will be significant only for the new big winners. So: number of big winners (or current returns on investment), new returns -> significance

chances of winning moderately and chances of winning really big; the latter is not a constant fraction of the former. What causes the correlation here? Wait, is there a correlation at all? PG says so. He says that big winners won’t seem like moderate winners. If you are more likely to be a big winner, then you won’t seem like a moderate winner. And, if you don’t seem like a moderate winner, you will have a small likelihood of being a big winner. So, why? I think what makes you likely to be a big winner makes you less likely to be a moderate winner.

x factor -> big winner; x factor -> less likely to be a moderate winner. So, if you don’t have that x factor, you won’t seem like a big winner and you will have a normal chance of being a moderate winner.

Harder - Correlations and Causes

quality of new startup ideas, how bad they seem at first. PG says: obviously good -> would have already been done (i.e., won’t be new). So, any new idea would have to be not obviously good. new idea -> successful idea; (all successful ideas would be new ideas, so they too would be not obviously good)

seems like a bad idea, is a good idea, how big you will succeed

ideas that seem bad, are bad vs good

best ideas seem like bad ideas, how hard it is to recognize the winners

retrospect, big successes seem obvious

Facebook, Microsoft, Apple - perfect bad ideas

[My analysis: how big you become, in terms of revenue or market share, depends on how much scarcity power you have (also on how big the market is).

scarcity power, market size -> bigness.

goodness of idea x how good it seems: bad and seems bad, bad but doesn’t seem bad, good and seems good, good but doesn’t seem bad

The only startups that can succeed are those with good ideas (whether they seem good or not). But, good ideas that seem good (to many people) will have a lot of people behind them. So, even if your idea is good, you won’t have much scarcity power, and thus won’t be able to become really big. However, if you go after good ideas that seem bad, you at least have a chance of being the only game in town and becoming big (assuming that your market is big).

Also, if you’re in tune with the newest startups, you may find the moderately successful ideas to be good, but still find the really big ideas to be bad.]

good idea -> can succeed

good idea, seems bad -> scarcity power

in tune with latest trends -> most good ideas will seem good (but the best ones still won’t)

Conclusion

First, identify the correlations. And then investigate causes.

At each step, ask if it is a correlation or a cause.

For every variable, ask what its cause is.

Further Questions

Exactly how do you determine whether A causes B? What conditions do you need to satisfy? I get the feeling you need to show that P(B|A,X) = P(B|A) for all X. That is, knowing A screens off the effects of all non-descendants. But, what does that require, in non-math terms?

Say A and B are binary variables. What if A=on makes B=on, but A=off doesn’t say anything about B? How do you represent that as a causal link? What if C=on is what makes B=off (when A=off)? How do you show that?
